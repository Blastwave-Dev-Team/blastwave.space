Guide to Chemistry

Chemistry is the science of mixing reagents to create medicines, explosives, and other useful (or dangerous) compounds. This guide covers the basics of operating the ChemMaster 3000 and common recipes used aboard Redline Station.

Getting Started

Your workspace in the chemistry lab contains everything you need:

  • Chemical Dispenser — dispenses base reagents from energy
  • ChemMaster 3000 — separates, purifies, and packages reagents into pills or bottles
  • Chemical Heater — heats beakers to trigger temperature-sensitive reactions
  • Large Beaker — holds up to 100u of reagents

To begin, grab a beaker from the table and insert it into the chemical dispenser. Select your desired reagents and dispense them in the correct ratios.

Basic Medicines

The following table lists essential medicines every chemist should know:

Medicine Recipe Effect
Bicaridine Carbon + Oxygen + Sugar Heals brute damage at 2u/tick
Kelotane Silicon + Carbon + Nitrogen Heals burn damage at 2u/tick
Dylovene Silicon + Potassium + Nitrogen Purges toxins, heals tox damage
Dexalin Oxygen + Plasma (5:1) @ 350K Restores blood oxygenation
Tricordrazine Bicaridine + Kelotane + Dylovene Heals all damage types slowly

Temperature-Sensitive Reactions

Some reactions require specific temperatures. Use the chemical heater to bring your beaker to the target temperature before adding the final catalyst.

Tip: Always check the heater's readout before adding reagents. An uncontrolled exothermic reaction in an enclosed space can be lethal.

Thermite

A powerfulite compound that burns through almost any surface:

Iron (1u) + Aluminium (1u) + Oxygen (1u) = Thermite (3u)
Apply to floor/wall, then ignite with a lighter or welder.
Security Notice
Thermite is considered contraband. Unauthorized synthesis will result in a security response.

Advanced Compounds

Once you've mastered the basics, you can attempt more complex synthesis chains. These typically involve multiple intermediate reagents:

  1. Synthesize your base reagents (bicaridine, kelotane, etc.)
  2. Combine intermediates in the correct order — order matters for some reactions
  3. Heat or cool to the required temperature range
  4. The product will appear in your beaker once conditions are met

For a complete list of all available reactions, consult the Advanced Chemistry Reference or ask your Chief Medical Officer for guidance.
